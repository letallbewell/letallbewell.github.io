Lorentz Transformation

The Galilean transformation between reference frames moving with constant velocity from each other is straighforward: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} x^{'} &= x - vt, y^{'} = y, z^{'} = z, \text{ and},\\ t^{'} &= t. \end{aligned} \tag{1-1} \end{equation} However, this transformation is at odds with the behaviour of light that led to the postulate of special relativity.

Postulate of Special Relativity: All laws of physics are the same of all inertial reference frames.

Conversly, there is nothing an observer can do to differentiate between inertial frames. Most books add a second postulate stating that the speed of light, $c$, is constant for all inertial observers but it is a law of physics for me. We cannot outrun light unlike a fighter jet going past Mach 1 and Lorentz transformations must be changed.

There are a few things we can straightaway say about the new transformations:
  1. They are linear maps in $x$ and $t$ so that straight lines are mapped to straight lines so that law of inertia holds across frames.
  2. $ x = vt $ is mapped to the origin of the transformed coordinate.
Or equivalenty: $$ x^{'} = A (x - vt) $$ and $$ t{'} = Bx + Ct.$$ Space transformation

Going back to our premise, we want light to move with same speed in both frames, that is $ x = ct $ and $ x^{'} = ct^{'} $. This implies, $$ ct^{'} = A (c - v) t .$$ Taking the reverse transformation from the primed coordinates, $$ ct = A (c + v) t^{'} .$$ In general, $A$ can be a function of $v$ as we will shortly see. But it can only depend on the magnitude of the velocity to respect the homogeinity of space allowing us to use the same $A$ for the forward and reverse transformations.

Multiplying the transformations together, $$ c^{2}tt^{'} = A^{2} (c^{2} - v^{2}) tt^{'} ,$$ giving, $$ A = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^{2}}{c^{2}}}} .$$ To keep up with convention we will use $ \gamma $ or $ \gamma_{v} $ instead of $A$.

Time transformation

The time transformation equation is hiding in its space counterpart and we can get it out by simulataneously using the forward and backward transforms. \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} x &= \gamma ( x^{'} + vt^{'}) \\ &= \gamma ( \gamma (x - vt) + vt^{'}) \\ &= \gamma^{2}x - \gamma^{2} vt + \gamma vt^{'}, \end{aligned} \end{equation*} or, $$ (1 - \gamma^{2}) x = -\gamma^{2}vt + + \gamma vt^{'}.$$ Noting that $ 1 - \gamma^{2} = -\frac{\gamma^{2}v^{2}}{c^{2}} $, cancelling a factor $ \gamma v $, we have the time transformation equation as, $$ t^{'} = \gamma \left(t - \frac{vx}{c^{2}} \right) .$$ Combining both transformations, Lorentz transformation can be summed up as \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} x^{'} &= \gamma (x - vt), y^{'} = y, z^{'} = z, \text{ and}, \\ t^{'} &= \gamma \left(t - \frac{vx}{c^{2}} \right), \\ \text{where } \gamma &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^{2}}{c^{2}}}} . \end{aligned} \tag{1-2} \end{equation}
